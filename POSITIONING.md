# Positioning

## Why This Exists

PSF needs two related but different messages:

- an internal business goal for us
- a public promise for climbers and gyms

They should point in the same direction, but they do not need to sound the same.

## Internal Business Goal

Build the world's most trusted and useful indoor climbing audience, then turn that audience into a durable media, sponsorship, and community platform.

More concretely:

- own the relationship with climbers
- become the default channel gyms want to use
- grow city-by-city, then country-by-country, then globally
- keep the audience engaged enough that the asset becomes hard to replace

## Public Promise

Suggested direction:

- the easiest way to stay in the loop on indoor climbing
- the place where your local climbing scene gets seen

Alternate sharper version:

- the world's best indoor climbing scene inbox

## What PSF Is Really Selling

PSF is not just events.

It is:

- local utility
- community visibility
- scene identity
- connection to what matters

## Content Hierarchy

The newsletter should have a clear hierarchy so the product does not lose its core premise.

### 1. Core Local Utility

This stays the main focus.

Examples:

- comps
- socials
- recurring events
- theme nights
- major gym updates
- setting changes that matter
- notable deadlines or reminders

### 2. Community Layer

This makes the product feel alive and worth opening.

Examples:

- gym owner or staff interviews
- coach or setter spotlights
- climber spotlights
- photo or video of the week
- local wins or interesting scene moments
- training tips

### 3. Global Layer

This is allowed, but it should stay secondary.

Examples:

- funny climbing content
- interesting newsworthy items
- broader scene stories
- global pieces that are easy to source and add flavour

Rule:

- global content should support the brand, not take it over

## What To Click Vs What To Consume

Not every item needs to be fully expanded in the email.

### Good Inline / Consume In Email

- short event reminders
- recurring event mentions
- quick setting updates
- simple community notes
- compact “what’s new” items

### Good Click-Through Items

- bigger events
- longer interviews
- detailed training tips
- feature stories
- photo or video galleries
- extended gym spotlights

The goal is to keep the email scannable while still creating pathways for deeper engagement.

## MVP Shape

For MVP, keep the product simple:

- one weekly send
- Melbourne first is acceptable at launch
- other cities can live on a waitlist or coming-soon model
- add community content only where it does not blow out workload
- use global content sparingly as seasoning, not the main course

## Feedback Loop

We should let the audience tell us what they want more of.

Things to test:

- how much people value event depth vs broader scene content
- how granular the city structure should be
- whether recurring events deserve more prominence
- whether setting updates are a core reason to open
- whether community submissions increase retention

## Business Success Metric

The best version of PSF is not just “people subscribe.”

It is:

- climbers trust it
- gyms want to be featured
- people open it habitually
- the audience becomes valuable enough to support sponsorship and future products
